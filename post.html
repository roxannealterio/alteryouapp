Progressive overload: the only rule that matters

By Roxanne Alterio5 min read

If you take one thing from anything we ever write, take this. The reason a program works is not that it left you wrecked, or that you sweated, or that you could barely walk down the stairs the next day. It works because, week over week, you asked your body to do a little more than it did last time. That is progressive overload, and it is the entire game.

Soreness is feedback. It is not a scorecard.

Doing more can look like a lot of things. An extra rep at the same weight. The same reps with two more kilos. One more set. A slower, more controlled tempo on the way down. A fuller range of motion than you managed a month ago. All of them are progress, and none of them require you to be destroyed by the end of the session.

Why soreness fools everyone

Muscle soreness mostly tells you that you did something your body was not used to, not that you did something effective. A brand new movement will make you sore whether or not it builds anything. And the movements you have done for months, the ones actually driving your results, often stop making you sore at all. If you chase the ache, you will keep swapping exercises to feel it, and quietly never get stronger at any of them.

What to do instead

Pick a program and stay on it long enough to add load. Write down what you lifted. Next time, try to beat it by something small and specific. When a weight starts to feel easy for all your reps, that is the signal to go up, not to add another finisher. This is exactly why ALTER nudges you to go heavier when your logged sets say you are ready, and why every program tells you how many weeks it needs before you begin.

Strength is built in boring, repeatable increments. That is not the disappointing part. That is the reassuring part, because it means it does not depend on motivation, or a perfect week, or how sore you got. It depends on showing up and adding a little. You can do that.
